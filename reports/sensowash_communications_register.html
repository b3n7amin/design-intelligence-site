01 — Diagnosis

The Register SensoWash Has vs. The Register It Needs

SensoWash is a product built entirely around body experience — warmth, flow, sensation, comfort. Its current communications speak a different language: features, specifications, hygiene corrections. The gap between what the product does and how the brand describes it is the central problem this guide addresses.

"A product that warms, cleanses, and cares for the body should not communicate the way a technical manual does."

Core finding — Duravit Brand Review, 2026
How SensoWash communicates now
–Feature-first structure: what the product has, then what it does
–Hygiene as correction — cleanliness framed as a problem being solved
–Specification language: pressure settings, temperature ranges, oscillation patterns
–Object-centred: the product acts, the body receives passively
–Design described visually only — "seamless," "integrated," "elegant"
–Wellness as a claim category, not felt experience
What the register needs to carry
+Sensation-first structure: how it feels before what it does
+Hygiene as care — cleanliness as confidence and wellbeing
+Body language: warmth, rhythm, ease, trust, reset
+Person-centred: the experience belongs to the user, not the product
+Design felt through use — precision experienced in the body, not just the eye
+Wellness earned through specific, honest moments
02 — The Strategic Shift

Moving the Language Without Moving the Brand

The change in register does not ask SensoWash to become a different brand. It stays fully within the Duravit frame — the precision, the design authority, the European restraint. The shift is in emphasis: from what the product contains to what the person experiences.

What stays the same

Duravit's precision and quality signals remain intact. The brand voice retains its authority, its restraint, and its design-led perspective. Nothing is added that cannot be substantiated by the product.

What changes

The order of communication shifts. Sensation and experience lead. Features and specifications follow. The person using the product comes before the product itself in every piece of copy.

What this unlocks

SensoWash reaches the emotional territory it already occupies in the bathroom. Buyers respond to the product's actual promise — comfort, warmth, daily care — rather than a technical specification they cannot yet feel.

The register shift in one sentence per layer

Communication Layer From To
Product story "Here is what this product has." "Here is what this product does to your morning."
Design language "A seamlessly integrated form." "Design that disappears — until you need it."
Hygiene claim "Promotes hygiene and wellbeing." "Clean that you feel, not just believe."
Precision signal "Engineered for exact performance." "Precision you register at skin level."
Comfort positioning "Adjustable comfort settings." "Adapts to you — nothing to think about."
03 — Language Principles

Five Rules for How SensoWash Should Sound

These principles apply across all SensoWash communications — product copy, campaign headlines, retail material, press assets, and digital content. They do not prescribe tone rigidly. They set the boundary conditions within which writers and creatives should work.

1

Lead with the body, not the button

Describe what a person feels before describing what the product does. The feature exists to create an experience. Lead with the experience.

This does not mean ignoring specification. It means writing in the right order. Sensation first. Mechanism second. It matches how buyers actually decide: they imagine the experience, then look for the reassurance of specification to justify it.

Test: Does the first sentence describe sensation or function? If function — reorder.
2

Design is felt, not just seen

Duravit's design authority is real and earned. For SensoWash, it should be communicated through how the product sits in use — how it fits the body, how it holds the room — not only through visual description.

Avoid design language that describes appearance without connecting it to experience. "Seamless" alone tells the eye. "Nothing to reach for, nothing in the way" tells the body.

Test: Is the design claim something a person would feel, or only see? Aim for both.
3

Warmth is not softness

The SensoWash register can carry warmth — human, specific, grounded in the body — without losing Duravit's authority or precision. Warmth does not mean casual, conversational, or brand-friendly. It means the writing acknowledges that a real person, in a real body, is at the centre of the experience.

The product is warm in temperature and in emotional quality. The copy should match both without over-reaching into lifestyle language that belongs to another brand.

Test: Would this copy feel out of place on a Duravit showroom wall? If yes — recalibrate.
4

Hygiene is confidence, not correction

The current register frames cleanliness as a problem the product solves. The shift is to frame it as a quality of experience the product delivers. Confidence, ease, and the quiet certainty that comes from genuine bodily care.

This is not a minor semantic point. It changes how the product makes people feel about themselves — and that determines whether it reaches the buyers whose values it matches most closely, particularly in wellness-led and premium home markets.

Test: Does the hygiene claim feel corrective or caring? If corrective — reframe.
5

The moment is the message

Abstract product claims — "innovation," "wellbeing," "advanced technology" — say nothing about the product and less about the person. Grounding communications in a specific moment (the start of the day, the reset before sleep, the quiet five minutes alone) makes the claim specific, credible, and memorable.

Specificity does not narrow the audience. It makes the product more recognisable to the exact people who already want it.

Test: Could this copy apply to any shower toilet? If yes — make it more specific to the moment.

05 — Before / After

How the Register Change Reads in Practice

These rewrites apply the five principles to typical SensoWash copy situations. Each includes a note explaining what changed and why. Use these as reference points when reviewing or producing SensoWash content.

Core product headline
Current

"SensoWash — The shower toilet with integrated cleansing system and heated seat."

Revised

"SensoWash — The bathroom that knows what your body needs before you do."

Change: Moves from specification list to promise. Sensation and intelligence replace features. The body is centred. Duravit's precision is implied, not stated.
Spray / cleansing function
Current

"Oscillating spray technology for thorough hygiene cleaning with adjustable pressure and temperature."

Revised

"A gentle rhythm that does more than you'd expect. Clean you feel, not just believe — always at the warmth and strength that fits you."

Change: "Oscillating" is a mechanical term with no felt quality. "Rhythm" carries the same technical reality in sensory language. "Clean you feel" replaces the corrective hygiene frame. Adjustability becomes personal fit.
Heated seat
Current

"Heated toilet seat with adjustable temperature settings for added comfort."

Revised

"Warmth that meets you — not something you notice, just something you'd miss if it were gone."

Change: "Added comfort" is the weakest possible descriptor for a product feature built entirely on physical sensation. The revision creates a felt memory: the warmth is so natural it disappears — which is precisely the quality worth communicating.
Design language
Current

"A seamlessly integrated design that complements any bathroom aesthetic with refined, minimalist form."

Revised

"SensoWash earns its place by fitting — into the room, into the routine, into the body's logic. The design is invisible until it matters."

Change: "Seamlessly integrated" is a visual claim. The revision keeps Duravit's design authority while extending it into use — how the product fits space, habit, and the body. This is the design language SensoWash specifically needs.
Wellness / health positioning
Current

"Promotes hygiene and wellbeing with advanced cleaning technology for a healthier lifestyle."

Revised

"The kind of care you didn't know a bathroom could offer. SensoWash turns five minutes alone into a real reset — every morning, every evening."

Change: "Promotes hygiene" is a corrective claim. "Healthier lifestyle" is a category cliché. The revision grounds wellness in a specific daily moment and makes the benefit personal, not clinical. "Care" replaces "hygiene" as the emotional frame.
Duravit heritage signal
Current

"Engineered with Duravit precision and quality standards for long-lasting performance."

Revised

"Built with the same intention Duravit brings to every surface. SensoWash holds that standard where it matters most — at the point of contact between the product and the person."

Change: The original is a generic quality claim. The revision connects Duravit's precision directly to the SensoWash experience — specificity at the point of physical contact. It earns the heritage reference rather than borrowing it.
Air dryer function
Current

"Integrated warm air dryer with three temperature levels for a complete hygiene experience."

Revised

"The routine ends as it should — unhurried, dry, complete. Nothing to reach for."

Change: "Complete hygiene experience" is a clinical summary. The revision describes the moment: the end of a routine that asks nothing of you. "Nothing to reach for" is the emotional payoff of the dryer function expressed in human terms.
06 — Vocabulary Guide

Words That Belong in the Register — and Words That Don't

This is not an exhaustive list. It is a calibration tool. When a writer is uncertain whether a word fits, this guide offers the clearest indication of direction.

Use — words that carry the register
feel warmth rhythm ease restore adapt trust quiet moment skin ritual care natural confident calm precise held reset body contact complete unhurried intention earned

These words are earned when they are specific and grounded in product reality. Avoid using them as decoration.

Avoid — words that work against the register
hygiene cleanliness oscillating advanced technology features settings promotes thorough integrated seamless innovative state-of-the-art premium long-lasting added comfort healthier lifestyle

These are not inherently wrong words. They belong to the category-wide register — not the SensoWash-specific one. They signal the product has not earned its own language yet.

A note on "hygiene"

The word "hygiene" carries a clinical frame that is difficult to escape in SensoWash communications. It implies that the body is a problem being corrected. The product is not a hygiene device — it is a care device. When hygiene must be referenced (regulatory copy, technical specs), use it precisely and sparingly. In all other communications, replace it with care, confidence, cleanliness felt from the inside, or the specific experience the feature creates.

07 — Channel Notes

How the Register Modulates by Channel and Market

The five principles hold across all channels. The weight and emphasis shift depending on context, audience, and how close the person is to a purchase decision. These notes give the brand team practical guidance for each touchpoint.

Website — Product Pages

Body-first headline. Sensation and experience in the opening paragraph. Product specifications in a secondary or expandable section. Photography should carry the warmth and body signals that the copy then reinforces — not substitute for them.

Rule: If the headline could apply to any shower toilet, rewrite it.
Retail & Showroom

The product speaks before the copy does in a showroom setting. Text should honour that: short, felt, specific. Avoid full feature lists on display materials. Use the register to create the desire to try — then let the product confirm it.

Rule: "Try before you read" — copy supports the experience, never replaces it.
Press & Trade Media

Maintain Duravit's design authority tone for architecture and contract press. For lifestyle, wellness, and consumer media, lead with the sensory register. The same product story, told at different points of emphasis for different editorial contexts.

Rule: Match the body-first register to the outlet's existing wellness and design language.
Social & Content

Moment-led. Daily rituals, specific times of day, the quality of five minutes without interruption. Avoid feature announcements in social copy. The register here is the most personal and least formal — still within the Duravit frame, but closest to conversation.

Rule: Every post should describe a moment, not a product.
Trade & Specification

Technical and specification copy serves a different purpose — it justifies a decision already made on experiential grounds. Use precise, professional language here without apology. The register does not apply to datasheets. It applies to everything that precedes them.

Rule: Specs confirm; they don't sell. Keep the register for communications that do.
Retail & Installer Briefing

The sales channel needs to understand and own the register before they can use it. Briefing materials for kitchen and bathroom specialists, interior designers, and contractors should explain the register shift in plain terms and provide example language for showroom conversations.

Rule: Give trade partners the words before expecting them to use them.

Market emphasis notes

The register shift applies globally, but the degree of sensory and emotional emphasis should flex by market. These notes reflect the distinct buyer motivations in SensoWash's priority regions.

Europe Core
Lead with design authority and quiet confidence. Sensory language is secondary — implied rather than stated. Warmth and care should come through product intelligence, not emotional declaration. Restraint is a strength in this context.
Gulf Region Priority
The strongest opportunity for the full sensory and desire register. Luxury wellness language is not only appropriate here — it is expected. Lead with warmth, care, and the quality of the private experience. Design authority reinforces rather than leads. This is the market where SensoWash's emotional language can go furthest within the Duravit frame.
United States Developing
Wellness and home investment language resonates. The register should connect sensory comfort to the growing wellness-at-home narrative without adopting lifestyle-brand tone. Design credentials matter but need translation into everyday benefit rather than European restraint.
China & Asia Pacific Caution
Duravit Blue reads as clinical in some Asian markets. Design authority framing is more effective than sensory warmth here. Precision, technology leadership, and status remain the primary motivators. The warmth register should be applied lightly and tested locally before broad deployment.
Closing Note

SensoWash already delivers the experience this register asks it to describe. The work is not invention — it is translation. The product has been speaking in features; this guide asks it to speak in feelings — without leaving behind the precision and design authority that made those feelings possible.

What success looks like

A buyer reads a SensoWash headline and recognises the experience before they have stood in the showroom. The product's promise is felt in language — then confirmed in person.

What to measure

Showroom dwell time and trial rates. Content engagement on sensory vs. feature-led posts. Qualitative feedback from trade partners on how buyers respond to new copy. Message recall in buyer research: can they describe what it felt like?
