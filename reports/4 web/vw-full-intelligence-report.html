The argument begins here
Before diagnosing what is wrong with Volkswagen, it is necessary to understand what is right — and what is genuinely distinctive. Because the diagnosis that follows is not that VW has the wrong identity. It is that VW has the right identity and is failing to protect it. That distinction matters enormously for what the solutions look like.
Section one

What Volkswagen actually stands for

VW's identity is among the most coherent of any volume automotive brand. It has a clear emotional centre, a distinctive communication style, and values that have held consistently across eight decades. The diagnostic finds no ambiguity about what the brand is.

Three promises VW makes — and how each one is performing
Dependable value — the most important promise and the most violated one
Products that hold their quality and their worth over time. The Atlas infotainment crisis (D·28) and the ID.4 recall sequence struck directly at this commitment with the audiences who trusted VW most. The brand promised reliability and delivered software failures. That is not a communication problem. It is a behaviour problem — and behaviour must be fixed before communication can help.
Technology that feels human — the most frequently violated in product decisions
Not technology for its own sake. Technology that is intuitive, accessible, and honest about what it does. The move to touch-only interfaces across multiple models between 2020 and 2024 was the single most damaging internal decision of the past decade. It contradicted this promise at the moment of use, every day, for every driver. The 2025–26 corrections are right — but they came late.
Understanding across life stages — the deepest claim, and the most credible with the right audience
The brand's most ambitious claim: that VW sees the person, not just the customer. Currently most credible with Group 1 (European family buyers, 35–55) and least credible with younger urban audiences who don't yet see the brand as relevant to the life stage they're in. The gap is not permanent — but it requires product and communication decisions that currently aren't being made.
The argument develops
If VW's identity is coherent, why are its resonance scores so poor? The answer is not that the brand is misunderstood. The answer is that the brand has made a series of commitments it has not kept, and that the audiences who most valued those commitments are the ones who feel the gap most acutely. The dissonance is not a perception problem. It is a delivery problem wearing a perception costume.

The pattern in both failures is identical: a UX philosophy — technology-first, screen-dominant, touch-only — was applied to products without checking it against the character of the buyers who would use them. The synthesis
The brand portrait, the audience scores, and the fleet analysis are three separate lenses on the same problem. The Resonance Ring brings them together into a single diagnostic picture: three relationships, three different health readings, one structural weakness that the data keeps pointing to.
Section four

The Resonance Ring — where the three connections hold and break

For a car brand to genuinely connect with buyers, three relationships must hold simultaneously: brand to user, brand to vehicle, vehicle to user. When all three align, the result is loyalty that persists through price increases and competitive alternatives. Right now, only one of VW's three connections is strong.

The argument goes deeper
The product failures and the communication inconsistencies documented above share a common structural cause: the organisation's decision-making layer is not governed by the brand's own character. The CEO's public register, the UX philosophy that produced the Atlas crisis, the strategy communications that contradict the campaign layer — all three are symptoms of the same absence: brand character is not the filter that product and leadership decisions pass through before they reach the market.
Section five

The risk emerges when the leader's personal identity accumulates enough external visibility that it begins displacing the brand's own character in how the world reads the company. Three governance actions — before the drift becomes irreversible
1
Run every CEO public statement through the brand's own communication filter before publication
The filter already exists in VW's own communication principles — "Disarmingly Honest / Genuinely Human." Apply it to CEO communications the same way it is applied to campaign copy. A press release that would fail the filter in a campaign brief should not appear under the VW brand in any context. This is a governance decision. No external resource required.
2
Create explicit protection for the brand's wit register at leadership level
Designate a senior brand leader as the explicit guardian of VW's self-aware, quietly funny communication register. Ensure at least one communication per quarter from leadership level demonstrates this register. The wit will not survive long-term without a sponsor at the level where budget and brief decisions are made.
3
Anchor one major Blume speech per year to the brand's warmth and heritage register
One keynote per year — at the most consumer-facing event — written from the CA + heritage + wit register: what ordinary people's relationship with VW has meant over 80 years. Not technology targets. Not market share goals. The relationships. This is the single most powerful external signal that the CEO understands what the brand genuinely is.

Section seven

The two failures that need solving — in full

Four structural gaps were identified across this report. Two of them are acute: the Atlas is generating active trust damage today, and the Golf Electric brief is being written now. The other two — China structural mismatch and communication register fragmentation — are addressed in the conclusion's priority sequence. These two come first because they are both time-sensitive.

At each formal design review — concept, alpha, beta, pre-production — a Group 1 panel of 8–10 people evaluates the current design against three questions: Does this feel honest? Does this feel easy? Does this feel like a VW? Any design element that fails two of three is flagged for revision before the gate passes. This is a governance mechanism, not a focus group. The panel's role is to catch drift before it becomes a product decision that costs hundreds of millions to reverse.

The conclusion of the argument
Eight sections. One argument. VW's identity is not the problem. The organisation's relationship with its own identity is the problem. Every finding in this report — the resonance scores, the fleet dissonance, the leadership drift, the competitive disadvantages — traces back to the same root: decisions made without checking them against what the brand genuinely is.
Conclusion

The overall verdict

Volkswagen is one of the few volume automotive brands in the world that has a genuine identity — not a positioning, not a tagline, but a real emotional character that has been consistent across eight decades, across markets, across product categories. Warmth. Wit. Honest scale. The companion, not the hero. That is genuinely rare. It is genuinely valuable. And right now, the organisation is working against it on multiple fronts simultaneously.

This is not a brand in crisis. It is a brand that is failing to govern itself against its own strongest asset. The gap between what VW genuinely is and what its product decisions, leadership communications, and strategy cycles have been saying is the entire story. Close that gap, and the resonance scores recover. Leave it open, and the identity that has sustained VW for 80 years slowly becomes background noise behind a technology ambition that the brand cannot authentically claim.
The verdict in four frames
What is working
The identity is architecturally sound
The brand's character — warmth, heritage, wit, honest scale — is coherent, distinctive, and 80 years deep. The campaign layer (YourWagen, True Volkswagen, the GTI advertising) correctly expresses it. The ID. Buzz proves it can survive the EV transition intact. The Golf and GTI prove it can sustain across product generations. These are the reference cases for everything that needs to be fixed.
What is broken
Two product failures and a governance absence
The Atlas (D·28) and ID.4 (D·35) failures share one cause: UX decisions were made without checking them against brand character. The leadership drift adds a second governance failure: strategy communications use a register that contradicts the brand's identity. Both failures are repairable — but only if the organisation decides that brand character is the filter through which product and leadership decisions must pass before they reach the market.
What is structurally limited
China Group 4 and the desire deficit
The Chinese progressive urban buyer's resonance score of −0.09 is not fixable by adding more VW-branded products. The brand's identity is structurally misaligned with what this group requires. The correct structural answer is a governed separate entity — not called Volkswagen — that can carry desire and forward energy without diluting the trust VW has built with the audiences where its identity does work. JETTA is the template at the value end. The premium end needs the same approach.
What is genuinely defensible
Three windows no competitor can take
The family trust segment, the heritage EV territory, and the wit-led communication register are all genuinely defensible — no competitor can replicate them without 60–80 years of cultural history. BYD is taking the desire and forward-drive territory. Toyota is competing in the warmth and reliability territory. The wit territory is entirely uncontested. VW is the only brand in the world with permission to be genuinely funny about what a car is. That is an extraordinary asset. It should be the most protected thing in the portfolio.
The four decisions that determine whether VW recovers or continues to drift
1
Lock the Golf Electric brief to the Golf GTI's character — before any other decision is made
This is the most time-sensitive decision in the entire report. The Golf Electric brief is being written now. If it is briefed from the Golf GTI's character — warm, witty, honest at human scale, physical controls as default — it will be VW's strongest EV and the product that proves the brand can survive the electrification transition with its identity intact. If it is briefed from technology-first, the ID.4 failure repeats at higher volume. There is no middle outcome. The window for the right decision is now.
⏱ Decision window: now, at brief stage · Cost if right: zero · Cost if wrong: the next ID.4
2
Fix the Atlas trust damage with behaviour — outreach, remediation, governance standard — before any campaign
VW's highest-trust audience is currently in marginal resonance territory (+0.15). The Atlas is the primary cause. The remediation plan is clear, the sequencing is clear, the expected outcomes are measurable. The one rule that must not break: communication comes after behaviour, not before it. Every campaign VW runs for Group 1 before the Atlas situation is visibly addressed makes the dissonance gap larger, not smaller, because it amplifies the gap between what the brand says and what owners are experiencing.
⏱ Timeline: 18 months · Priority: outreach in weeks, governance standard before next product cycle
3
Create a governed separate brand entity for the premium urban China segment — do not use the VW name
Adding more VW-branded products to the China market will not resolve the Group 4 resonance deficit. The identity mismatch is structural: what Group 4 requires (desire, aspiration, forward energy) is what VW's identity most suppresses. A separate brand entity — desire-forward character, Xpeng technology underneath, VW engineering standards as the quality backstop — is the structurally correct answer. JETTA at the value end has already proven the governance model works. The premium end needs the same approach, before a competitor with the right character occupies the territory permanently.
⏱ Timeline: 24–36 months to launch · Platform infrastructure already exists
4
Govern the CEO's public register against the brand's own communication filter — before the drift becomes irreversible
Leadership drift at 30 months is still correctable with a governance decision. At 48 months, the external signal — analysts, media, partners — will have built the "VW as technology ambition brand" narrative into the public record, and returning to the warmth register requires an active repositioning programme rather than a filter. Three specific actions: run every CEO public statement through the brand's own "Disarmingly Honest" filter, designate a senior leader as explicit guardian of the brand's wit register, and anchor one major Blume speech per year to the warmth and heritage register. The cost is minimal. The cost of inaction compounds every month.
⏱ Window: now — action in this quarter, not next year · Cost of acting now: minimal · Cost of acting in 18 months: significant
The single sentence that summarises the entire diagnosis
VW is losing ground not because its identity is weak, but because the organisation keeps making decisions as if the identity doesn't exist — product decisions that contradict its promises, leadership communications that speak a different language, and strategy cycles that overpromise on dimensions the brand cannot authentically inhabit.
Fix that governance failure, and the identity does the rest. The brand knows who it is. The question is whether the organisation will start listening to it.
